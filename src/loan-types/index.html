Understanding loan types

The type of mortgage you choose has a big impact on how much you end up paying – how much you owe upfront, your monthly payment amount, and the total cost of your loan over time. Knowing what type of loan is most appropriate for your situation prepares you for talking to lenders and getting the best deal.

A loan “option” is actually a combination of several different options:

  1. Loan term
  2. Interest rate structure
  3. Loan program

What to know

Shorter terms will generally save you money overall, but have higher monthly payments. There are two reasons shorter terms can save you money:

  1. You are borrowing money and paying interest for a shorter amount of time.
  2. The interest rate is usually lower — by as much as a full percentage point — with a shorter term than with a longer term.

Rates are different at different lenders, especially for shorter terms. Don’t rely on guesswork to make your decision. Always compare official loan offers (called Good Faith Estimates) to make sure you’re getting the right deal for you.

Some lenders, especially in rural areas, may offer balloon loans. With a balloon loan, the payments are calculated based on a longer term (typically 30 years) but the loan is actually due much earlier (often after 5 years). Learn more.

Interest rate structure

Fixed rate or Adjustable rate

Interest rates come in two basic types: fixed and adjustable.

This choice affects:

  • Whether or not your interest rate can change
  • Whether or not your monthly principal and interest payment can change
  • The amount of your monthly principal and interest payment
  • How much interest you will pay over the life of the loan

What are my choices for interest rate structure?

Fixed rate

  • Lower risk; No suprises
  • Higher interest rate
  • Rate does not change
  • Rate and payments stay the same
How Does It Work?

Your rate stays the same throughout the loan.

why might you choose this?

Fixed rates offer certainty throughout the life of the loan.

Your interest rate and monthly principal and interest payment will stay the same over the term of your loan.

Your total monthly payment (including property taxes, homeowner’s insurance, and mortgage insurance) can change over time. Learn more

Deciphering Adjustable Rate Mortgages (ARMs)

Most ARMs are structured into two separate periods. During the first period, your interest rate is fixed and won’t change. During the second period, your rate adjusts regularly based on market changes.

Sample Adjustable Rate Mortgage

5 / 1 ARM: Fixed period

This “5” represents how many years your inital interest rate will stay fixed.

The most common options are 3, 5, 7, and 10 years.

5 / 1 ARM: Adjustable period

This “1” represents how often your rate changes after the fixed period expires.

The most common option is 1, meaning the rate will adjust every year. At each one of these intervals, you would receive a new rate with a new payment amount.

The changes to your rate will be based on the market conditions. Learn more

Other, less common structures that follow this basic pattern include 5/5 (fixed for the first 5 years, adjusts every 5 years thereafter) and 3/3 (fixed for the first 3 years, adjusts every 3 years thereafter). ARMs typically have a 30-year loan term.

ARMs can have other structures.

Not all ARMs follow the above pattern. Some ARMs may adjust every 6 months, or even every 1 month, and there’s not a standard way that these are described. If you’re considering a nonstandard structure, make sure to carefully read the rules and ask questions about when and how your rate and payment can adjust.

Most ARMs include caps on how much your rate can adjust.

ARMs include specific rules that dictate how your new rate and payment will be calculated at each adjustment interval. Rate changes are calculated using a market index (benchmark) plus a lender’s profit margin, but not all lenders use the same index or charge the same margin. Most ARMs also include caps on how much your rate and payment can adjust. Make sure you understand how these rules work. If you don’t, ask questions!

ARMs marketed to people with lower credit scores tend to be riskier.

If you have a credit score in the mid-600s or below, you are more likely to be offered ARMs that contain features that are risky for you such as: higher rates, rates that adjust more frequently, pre-payment penalties, and loan balances that can increase. Consult with multiple lenders and get a quote for an FHA loan as well. Then, you can compare all your options.

Loan program

Learn More

Conventional, FHA, or Special programs

Loan options are organized into broad categories based on the size of the loan and whether or not they are part of a government program.

This choice affects:

  • How much you will need for a down payment
  • The total cost of your loan, including interest and mortgage insurance
  • The house price range you can consider

Choosing the right loan type

Each loan type is designed for different circumstances. Sometimes, only one loan type will fit your situation. If multiple options fit your situation, ask lenders to provide several quotes so you can see which type offers the best deal overall.

Conventional

Majority of loans

Conventional loans typically have lower costs than FHA loans but stricter standards for approval.

Get all the details

FHA

Low down payment; low credit score

FHA loans are regulated and guaranteed by the Federal Housing Administration (FHA), a government agency.

Get all the details

Special programs

  • VA: veteran, servicemember, or surviving spouse
  • USDA: Rural; low- to middle-income
  • Local: Low- to middle-income; first-time homebuyer; public service employee

Get all the details

All loans are subject to basic government regulation. Your lender must document and verify your income, employment, assets, debts, and credit history in order to determine that you can afford to repay the loan.

Ask lenders whether the loan they are offering you meets the government’s Qualified Mortgage standard. This standard identifies loans that are safest for you, the borrower.

If multiple options fit your situation, ask lenders to provide several quotes so you can see which type offers the best deal overall.
