Special Loan Progams

Special loan programs exist for borrowers that meet specific eligibility criteria. For those that qualify, these programs can often be more affordable than a conventional or FHA loan, so make sure to investigate if you are eligible. Always compare official loan offers (called Good Faith Estimates) before making a final decision.

VA loan

The Veterans Administration (VA) has a loan program for veterans, current servicemembers, and surviving spouses. The program is similar to the FHA program, but partially subsidized so it can offer a better deal for those who qualify. VA loans also offer low-cost, streamlined refinance options and some additional protections if you have trouble paying your mortgage later on.

Unlike FHA loans, VA loans do not require monthly mortgage insurance premiums, but usually require an upfront fee at closing. Learn more about mortgage insurance.

VA loans are available with low or even zero down payments, but may be more expensive than conventional loans. If you have significant down payment funds and good credit, you should compare VA and conventional loans to see which offers the better deal.

USDA loan (Or Rural Development Loan)

The US Department of Agriculture offers a similar program to the FHA and VA, designed specifically for low- and moderate-income borrowers in rural areas.

USDA loans can be a good option for qualifying borrowers who have little available savings. They offer zero down payments and are usually cheaper than FHA loans. Borrowers will pay an upfront fee as well as ongoing mortgage insurance premiums to the USDA.

State and Local Programs

Many states, local governments, and local nonprofits offer various programs to help make homeownership more affordable. Many of these programs focus on low- and moderate-income families buying their first home, though some may be available to families who have previously owned a home. Other programs target teachers, firefighters, and other public service employees, or people interested in purchasing a home in a particular neighborhood.

Many programs offer down payment assistance that can be used with a regular FHA or conventional loan. Some programs lend money directly through subsidized loans.

If you think you might qualify, explore programs in your area using this tool or concact a local housing counselor to discuss your options. You can also ask local lenders whether they offer any special programs.

Mortgage Insurance: What you need to know

Mortgage insurance helps you get a loan you wouldn’t otherwise be able to get. If you can’t afford a 20 percent down payment, you will likely have to pay for some kind mortgage insurance. You may choose to get a conventional loan with private mortgage insurance (PMI), or an FHA, VA, or USDA loan.

Mortgage insurance usually adds to your costs. Depending on the loan type, you will pay monthly mortgage insurance premiums, an upfront mortgage insurance fee, or both. Mortgage insurance is an important component of your total loan cost, but so is the interest rate, which varies widely depending on your loan type, down payment amount, and the specific lender. If you’re considering multiple options, ask lenders for quotes for each option and compare total costs to see which option offers the best overall deal.

Mortgage insurance—no matter what kind—protects the lender in the event that you fall behind on your payments. It does not protect you. Your credit score will suffer and you may face foreclosure if you don’t pay your mortgage on time.
