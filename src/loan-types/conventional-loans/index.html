Conventional loans

“Conventional” just means that the loan is not part of a specific government program. Conventional loans typically have lower costs than FHA loans but stricter standards for approval. There are two main categories of conventional loans–conforming and non-conforming.

Conforming loans have maximum loan amounts that are set by the government. Other rules for conforming loans are set by Fannie Mae or Freddie Mac, companies that provide backing for conforming loans).

Non-conforming loans are less standardized. Eligibility, pricing, and features can vary widely by lender, so it’s particularly important to shop around and compare several offers.

Conforming

$417,000 or less

  • The most common loan type.
  • The loan amount must be $417,000 or less, unless you’re buying a home with multiple units.
  • If your down payment is less than 20%, you’ll probably need mortgage insurance.

Conforming Jumbo

$417,000 to county limit

  • An extension of the conforming program for loan amounts higher than $417,000.
  • Only available in certain counties.
  • The maximum loan amount varies by county. Learn what the loan limit is in your county.

Non-conforming Jumbo

up to $1-2 million

  • Many lenders offer Jumbo loans for loan amounts greater than the Conforming Jumbo limit in your county.
  • You usually must have good credit and a high down payment to qualify.

Non-conforming (other)

  • Some loans in this category are intended for borrowers with poor credit. These loans tend to have high rates and may contain risky features.
  • Some lenders also offer niche programs for mainstream borrowers with unusual circumstances. These can include:
    • Loans for properties with non-standard features (such as more than 10 acres of land, properties with agricultural income, or properties that are difficult to appraise).
    • Loans for affluent customers with tricky finances, such as self-employed borrowers, or newly graduated doctors.

    Many of the subprime loans that got people in trouble during the crisis fell in the Non-conforming (other) category.

    Stronger government regulation means that many of these loans won’t return, but some will. Non-conforming loans marketed to people with lower credit scores are more likely to have high rates and contain risky features, such as rates that adjust more than once per year, prepayment penalties, and loan balances that can increase. If you are considering a non-conforming loan, consult with multiple lenders. Ask if you could qualify for a conforming or FHA loan instead. Never make a final decision about which loan to take before getting your official Good Faith Estimates.

    Mortgage Insurance: What you need to know

    Mortgage insurance helps you get a loan you wouldn’t otherwise be able to get. If you can’t afford a 20 percent down payment, you will likely have to pay for some kind mortgage insurance. You may choose to get a conventional loan with private mortgage insurance (PMI), or an FHA, VA, or USDA loan.

    Mortgage insurance usually adds to your costs. Depending on the loan type, you will pay monthly mortgage insurance premiums, an upfront mortgage insurance fee, or both. Mortgage insurance is an important component of your total loan cost, but so is the interest rate, which varies widely depending on your loan type, down payment amount, and the specific lender. If you’re considering multiple options, ask lenders for quotes for each option and compare total costs to see which option offers the best overall deal.

    Mortgage insurance—no matter what kind—protects the lender in the event that you fall behind on your payments. It does not protect you. Your credit score will suffer and you may face foreclosure if you don’t pay your mortgage on time.
